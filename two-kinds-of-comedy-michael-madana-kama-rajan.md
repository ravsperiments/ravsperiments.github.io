If you ask people to name the most comic character in [Michael Madana Kama Rajan](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0097813/), many will say Kameshwaran. But, Rajan is the one with a humor sense. Kameshwaran is perhaps the one who gets the most laughs, but only because he is unintentionally funny. He goes about his life with complete earnestness; it is this seriousness that appears funny to viewers. He is not innocent. He complains, sulks, and reacts without thinking, and that very lack of self-awareness — the inability to laugh at himself — becomes humorous.

Rajan, by contrast, is carefree and mischievous. He is entirely self-aware — he knows he is funny, and can laugh at himself

This distinction is already clear in the screenplay. But Kamal, being Kamal, does not settle for merely writing the correct answer. Like an overachieving student, he underlines it, circles it, and highlights it to make sure there is no possibility of missing the point.

Here’s how Kamal underlines the comic nature of Rajan’s character. After Rajan’s introductory sequence transitions into the film’s first song, **[Rum Bum Bum](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OOOuhERfRDU&list=RDOOOuhERfRDU&start_radio=1)**, he literally appears in a T-shirt printed with a cartoon character (Ernie from Sesame Street?). That should have been enough for most artists, but Kamal does not stop there. He highlights it further by having the song’s prelude bear an uncanny resemblance to the DuckTales theme.

I am, of course, assuming Kamal suggested this reference to Ilaiyaraaja. But that assumption is not without precedent. There are multiple anecdotes where Ilaiyaraaja has recalled Kamal’s references to some of his popular songs. A well-known instance is his suggestion of **“[Naan Paarthathile Aval Oruthiyai Thaan](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JajVgJyULGY&list=RDJajVgJyULGY&start_radio=1)”** as inspiration for **[Pudhu Maappillaiku](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CQe3mfCMf8s&list=RDCQe3mfCMf8s&start_radio=1)** in [Apoorva Sagodharargal](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0096895/). It is a mind-blowingly ironic reference — that he took a popular song associated with M. G. R - one of the biggest larger than life stars in Tamil cinema - as the reference point for a clown’s romance (how on earth did he even think of that?). Assuming that the same artistic instinct shaped these choices too is perhaps not too far-fetched.

Given the cue, Ilaiyaraaja plays it out of the park, crafting a delightful brass prelude that hints at the DuckTales theme but also something entirely his own. The main melody itself has the feel of a 1950s Western pop tune - simple, bright, and instantly hummable, but the star of the show is the trumpet. While the lead singers are wonderful, the trumpet almost behaves like a third singer. Every time it enters the song, it lifts the mood even further and takes the song’s energy into a higher orbit.

Interestingly though, the same Kamal plays it very differently when it comes to Kameshwaran. The most famous song in the movie is perhaps **[Sundari Neeyum Sundaran Naanum](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1PKBkpxHWSA&list=RD1PKBkpxHWSA&start_radio=1)**. Not only is it an amazing song, it is also brilliantly conceived and shot — perhaps one of the best filmed song sequences in Tamil cinema. But the song also reveals something deeper about Kameshwaran.

The song itself is a dream sequence. The film does not suggest whose dream it is, but I like to think it is Kameshwaran’s. In that dream, Kameshwaran is the exact opposite of his screen character — confident, romantic, and overtly masculine. The contrast is striking. The man who spends the film fussing and complaining about small inconveniences imagines himself as the kind of larger-than-life romantic hero Tamil cinema loves to portray.

And that, in a subtle way, reinforces the point: Kameshwaran is not funny because he takes himself way too seriously, but because the gap between his self-image and reality is what makes him funny.

In some ways he is like Appu from [Apoorva Sagodharargal](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0096895/). Appu in the circus is funny because of the literal gap between what he is physically and what he might have been. With Kameshwaran, that gap is not physical but entirely a matter of character.
